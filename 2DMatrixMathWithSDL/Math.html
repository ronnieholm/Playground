There's another way to write \(p\) called matrix form: $$p = \begin{bmatrix}x\ y\ 1\end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix}\vec u\\ \vec v\\ o \end{bmatrix} = x\vec u + y\vec v + o$$.

Translation

Given a point \((x, y)\) the goal is to move it to \((x+a, y + b)\) or expressed in terms of vectors (here we leave out the matrix to the right of the point):

INSERT GRAPHICS

$$\begin{bmatrix}x & y & 1\end{bmatrix} \rightarrow \begin{bmatrix}x + a & y + b & 1\end{bmatrix}$$

For this transform to work, we define a translation matrix \(T_{a,b}\). Think of it as an operator which can work on any \((x, y)\): $$\begin{bmatrix}x & y & 1\end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix}1 & 0 & 0\\0 & 1 & 0\\a & b & 1\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}1x + 0y + 1a & 0x + 1y + 1b & 0x + 0y + 1\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}x + a & y + b & 1\end{bmatrix}$$ Some characteristics of these matrices is that they're sparse (lots of zeros) and the last column is always \(\begin{bmatrix}0\\ 0\\ 1\end{bmatrix}\).

Scaling

Given a series of points we want to make objects smaller or bigger.

INSERT GRAPHICS

$$\begin{bmatrix}x & y & 1 \end{bmatrix}\rightarrow \begin{bmatrix}ax & by & 1\end{bmatrix}$$

To accompish this we define a scaling matrix \(S_{a, b}\)

$$\begin{bmatrix}x & y & 1\end{bmatrix}\rightarrow \begin{bmatrix}a & 0 & 0\\ 0 & b & 0\\ 0 & 0 & 1\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}ax & by & 1\end{bmatrix}$$

Scaling isn't as easy as it seems. Suppose we have a unit square with corners in \((-1,1)\), \((1,1)\), \((1,-1)\), and \((-1,-1)\), and that we with to go from 2 units on the each side to 4 units. By applying \(S_{a, b}\) this works as intended.

INSERT GRAPHICS

Suppose instead the unit box has coordinates \((1,3)\), \((3,3)\), \((3,1)\), and \((1,1)\). Here scaling is still correct, but the center of the unit box has moved. This happens because the box isn't centered around \((0,0)\) but rather \((2,2)\).

To achieve the desired effect of scaling, first we translate position to origin, then scale, and finally translate back to original position:

$$T_{-a,-b} S_{c,d} T_{a,b}$$

Rotation

Rotation is always around origin. Using the left-hand rule to do copping motion with the right hand to determine the positive direction.

INSERT GRAPHICS

While rotating \(\theta\) degrees, observe how the distance \(r\) remains constant as the point rotation from \((x,y)\) to \((x',y')\). From trigonometry, we know that

$$x = r\cos\phi$$ $$y = r\sin\phi$$

We also have identity sum formulas stating that

$$\sin(u + v) = \sin u \cos v + \cos u \sin v \\ \cos(u + v) = \cos u \cos v - \sin u \sin v$$

Thus:

$$x' = r\cos(\theta + \phi)\\ x' = r\cos \theta \cos \phi - r\sin \theta \sin \phi$$

Now substitute in the definition of \(x\) and \(y\) from above to get

$$x' = x \cos \theta - y \sin \theta$$

Similarly

$$y' = r\sin(\theta + \phi)\\ y' = r \sin \theta \cos \phi + r \cos \theta \sin \phi\\ y' = x \sin \theta + y \cos \theta $$

Rewriting to matrix form, the rotation matrix \(R_\theta\) around origin becomes

$$\begin{bmatrix}x & y & 1\end{bmatrix} \rightarrow \begin{bmatrix}x' & y' & 1\end{bmatrix}\\ \begin{bmatrix}x & y & 1\end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix}\cos\theta & \sin\theta & 0\\ -\sin\theta & \cos\theta & 0\\ 0 & 0 & 1\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}x' & y' & 1 \end{bmatrix}$$

Then like with scaling first translate point to origin, do rotation, then translate point back:

$$T_{-a,-b} R_\theta T_{a, b}$$

Conclusion

These matrices are collectively known as the elementary matrices of computer graphics:

$$T_{a,b} = \begin{bmatrix}1 & 0 & 0\\0 & 1 & 0\\a & b & 1\end{bmatrix} S_{a,b} = \begin{bmatrix}a & 0 & 0\\ 0 & b & 0\\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} R_\theta = \begin{bmatrix}\cos\theta & \sin\theta & 0\\ -\sin\theta & \cos\theta & 0\\ 0 & 0 & 1\end{bmatrix}$$
